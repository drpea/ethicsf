2.01 AI in Diagnostic Medicine
Ethical considerations of artificial intelligence in healthcare
🔑 Core Concept AI is a double-edged sword — it can improve or worsen healthcare equity. Clinicians must guide, not blindly follow AI.
⚠️ Key Ethical Concerns
ConcernKey Points
Algorithmic BiasAI trained on non-representative data amplifies existing inequalities (e.g., pulse oximeters underperform in darker-skinned patients)
TransparencyAI operates in ways that are unclear/not replicable
ExplainabilityTrade-off: more accurate = less explainable
AccountabilityWho is responsible if AI fails? Clinician? Hospital? Developer?
PrivacyAI processes vast sensitive data; breaches erode trust
EquityAI may widen digital divide or bridge gaps if wisely implemented
⚖️ Ethical Principles for AI
J
Justice
AI tools must be fairly distributed; prevent further inequities
A
Autonomy
Patients must be informed about AI's role in their care
B
Beneficence/Non-maleficence
AI must yield measurable better outcomes to justify added risk/cost
Ac
Accountability
AI decisions must be explainable; responsibility clearly assigned
👨‍⚕️ Clinician Roles in Ethical AI Use
  • Human Oversight — AI = assistant; Clinician = authority. Never blindly follow AI output
  • Informed Communication — Explain AI's role to patients clearly and non-overwhelmingly
  • Accountability — Remain responsible for outcomes; verify AI against clinical judgment
  • Avoid over-reliance — Understand AI's limits and weaknesses
🔬 Algorithmic Bias — Key Facts
  • Arises from non-representative training datasets
  • Biases in medicine predate AI — rooted in research/political shortcomings
  • AI echoes and amplifies pre-existing human biases
  • Solution: Diverse, representative datasets; local data generation
  • Philippine dilemma: Can't use local data without consent → AI trained on Western datasets performs poorly
🛡️ How to Ethically Implement AI

Reduce Bias

  • Use diverse, representative datasets
  • Generate prospective, ethically obtained local data

Ensure Transparency

  • AI must be explainable to clinicians + patients
  • Balance accuracy vs explainability

Protect Privacy

  • Strong data security measures
  • Robust governance for data access/storage

Champion Equity

  • AI must be accessible to all, not only well-funded settings
  • Address cost + infrastructure barriers

Maintain Oversight

  • Human must supervise AI outputs
  • AI complements, not replaces, clinical judgment

Assign Accountability

  • Clearly delineate: clinician, hospital, developer, insurer, patient responsibility
⚠️ Exam Trap AI does NOT introduce bias — it amplifies pre-existing biases from medical research. Biases are rooted in human shortcomings that predate AI.

2.03 Decision Making in Children
Surrogate decision-making, Best Interest Standard vs Zone of Parental Discretion
🔑 Core Distinction: Competence vs Capacity Competence = legal status (determined by courts/law, e.g., age of majority) | Capacity = clinical determination by physician at the bedside (task-specific, can change)
⬆️ Hierarchy of Decision-Making Standards for Incompetent Patients
1
Previously Expressed Wishes / Advance Directives
Patient's own documented wishes when still competent — highest priority
2
Substituted Judgment
Surrogate decides based on what the patient WOULD HAVE wanted, considering past values
3
Best Interest Standard (BIS) — Lowest/Last Resort
Applied when patient has NEVER been competent; focuses on pain avoidance, well-being, survival

Best Interest Standard (BIS)

  • Seeks OPTIMAL outcome
  • Maximizes child's best interest
  • Justifies overriding parental choices
  • Principle of beneficence

❌ Criticized for:

  • Vague, ill-defined, inflexible
  • Assumes only ONE best option
  • Neglects family interests
  • Cannot function as limiting principle

Zone of Parental Discretion (ZPD) ⭐

  • Sets a HARM THRESHOLD (not optimal)
  • Tolerates good-enough decisions
  • Respects parental authority
  • Considered superior to BIS by many ethicists

✓ ZPD Ethical Claims:

  • Parental Right to make decisions based on values
  • No Obligation to maximize every benefit
  • Harm Threshold — override ONLY if significant harm
ZPD 2-Stage Test Algorithm
Parental decision made
↓
STAGE 1: Does carrying out the parental decision cause PROBABLE SIGNIFICANT HARM to the child?
↙ NO
Tolerate decision
Accept, don't advocate
↘ YES
STAGE 2: Does OVERRIDING the decision cause MORE harm than the original decision?
↙ YES
Choose lesser harm
Negotiate, educate
↘ NO
Override parental
decision
⚖️ Gillick Competence
Landmark UK case (1985) Minors CAN consent to medical treatment if they demonstrate sufficient maturity and understanding of the decision. Parental authority diminishes as child's competence increases.
  • Clinicians can treat without parental consent if child is Gillick competent
  • Doctor can maintain confidentiality even from parents
  • Fraser Guidelines: assess minor's capacity for contraceptive advice
🧩 Issues in Pediatric Decision-Making
IssueDescription

Involuntary AltruismProcedure done primarily for another's benefit (e.g., sibling organ donation) — ethically questionable
Asymmetrical RelationshipsChild's interests inseparable from family; focusing only on child = misplaced individualism

⚠️ Key Exam Points (1) BIS = LOWEST in hierarchy, used only when patient has NEVER been competent. (2) ZPD focuses on HARM, not optimal outcome. (3) Gillick competence = minor can consent if sufficiently mature. (4) Pediatrician CANNOT forcibly vaccinate — educate first; override only when harm threshold is crossed.

💭 Toombs: Lived Experience of Disability
Key concept: Phenomenology Body ≠ mere object. "I exist or live my body." The body is our orientational locus — how we relate to the world around us.

Loss of Mobility = Lived Body Disruption:

  • Change in character of surrounding space
  • Altered awareness/interaction with objects
  • Disruption of corporeal identity
  • Disturbance in relations with others
  • Change in temporal experience
Shame in Disability Non-rational response. "Being-for-the-Other" — seeing oneself through others' eyes. Also: frustration of intentionality — "I can no longer do → I must depend on others."
Temporal Experience Progressive disability: Physical demands ground one in the present. Alternatively, the future assumes overriding significance (fear of deterioration).

IssueBias
Organ TransplantationPWDs denied equal access based solely on disability; families pressured into donation

⚠️ Shakespeare's "Disability Paradox"

Disabled individuals often report high quality of life comparable to non-disabled people — contradicting external assumptions of tragedy. Disability = predicament, not tragedy or pure social construction.

🔑 Key Distinction: Impairment vs Disability Impairment = bodily/medical condition | Disability = social consequences (exclusion, discrimination, lack of accessibility). Bioethics often wrongly medicalizes disability by ignoring social context.
2.05 Biopower in Medicine: Control of Reproduction
Power, medicalization, and normative views on contraception

Naturalist (Biomedical) View

  • → Reproduction = biological process/state
  • → Value-neutral; descriptive facts
  • → Physiology, hormones, fertilization
  • → Championed by biomedical sciences
  • → Contraception counseling = facts (mechanism, effectiveness, risks)

Normative View

  • → Reproduction = subjective human experience
  • → Value-laden; personal/social/cultural/spiritual
  • → Uses terms: "procreation," "childbearing"
  • → Championed by feminism, religion
  • → Moral meaning, responsibility, implications
🏛️ Power & Biopower (Foucault/Arnason)
Power = ability to influence others to act
Biopower = "governance of life" — how medicine/institutions shape what is possible for bodies and lives, often without overt coercion

Modern power operates by (Arnason):

  • Prohibiting
  • Producing norms
  • Managing bodies/lives
  • Regulating populations

Sites: Access & institutional structures | Clinical technologies (e.g., the Pill) | Norms/morality (religious, socio-political)

🎗️ The Pill: Two Normative Strands

Feminist Strand

Women must control reproduction to secure autonomy, health, and social equality. BUT: contraception can become a gendered burden — responsibility falls disproportionately on women.

Catholic Strand (Humanae Vitae)

Rejects artificial contraception; proposes Natural Family Planning. Emphasizes moral unity of sexual behavior and reproduction; conjugal love.

Both strands are NORMATIVE — they shape what is considered "treatable, permissible, and responsible" at individual and population levels.
💊 Medicalization

Definition: Process by which a domain of life becomes organized through medical categories, professional authority, and clinical pathways.

"GOOD" Forms (Empowering)

  • Fertility control (pills/IUDs) for self-determination
  • Pain management for chronic conditions
  • Epilepsy, Alzheimer's as medical conditions
  • Supportive relationship counseling

"BAD" Forms (Disempowering)

  • Creating "inauthentic" love with drugs
  • Antidepressants to tolerate abusive relationship
  • Disease mongering (expanding diagnoses for profit)
  • Medicalizing normal female processes
Conscientious Objection Medical professionals may refuse a valid intervention when personal values conflict. BUT they must: be transparent, offer referral to qualified provider, and NOT abandon the patient.
2.06 Ethical Issues in Human Reproduction: ART
IVF, surrogacy, designer babies, feminist perspectives on ART
🔑 Most Important Principle Respect for Human Dignity — most important when dealing with infertile couples and ART. Parenthood should be desired before any intervention.

IVF Key Facts

  • Success rate: <25 per 100 women bring child to term
  • Age 40-42: only 10.6% success
  • Under 35: 28.2% success
  • Women = primary targets/burden
  • Not covered by PhilHealth

Fundamental Principles (ART)

  • Primacy of patient welfare
  • Patient autonomy (informed shared decision)
  • Social justice (equity regardless of race/gender/SES)
  • Avoid overly optimistic atmosphere — masks failure rates

Gene Dysregulation Risk

  • Imprinting phenotypes increased in ART (Silver-Russell, Beckwith-Wiedemann, Angelman)
  • Risk low (<1/5000) but real
  • Epigenetic changes during hormonal stimulation + embryo culture
  • PGD/PGS can screen these
🤰 Gestational Carrier (Surrogacy) — Ethical Issues

Ethical Questions:

  • Altruism vs commodification of reproduction?
  • What if the carrier bonds with the baby?
  • Citizenship of child (cross-border)
  • Financial compensation = almost always involved
  • Truly altruistic arrangements are RARE

Case: Convenience Surrogacy (CEO Case)

Requesting a gestational carrier purely for convenience (not medical necessity) = commercialization and commodification of reproduction
Cross-Border Surrogacy: Most careful evaluation needed for citizenship and legal parentage of the child
🧬 Designer Babies / Embryo Screening
ConceptDetails
PGSPre-implantation Genetic Screening — screens for chromosomal abnormalities
PGDPre-implantation Genetic Diagnosis — diagnoses specific genetic conditions; can be used for sex selection
CRISPR"Genetic Swiss Army Knife" — cuts/rewrites DNA; rapid and cheap; heritable changes pass to all future generations
Ethical LimitScreening for medically indicated purposes = OK; selection for NON-medical traits (intelligence, appearance) = ethically problematic
⚡ "Fertility is a Feminist Issue" — Key Points
  • ART was supposed to liberate women but introduced NEW physical, legal, social burdens
  • Once embryos exist in vitro, woman's "special rights" based on gestation diminished
  • IVF success rate: fewer than 25/100 women bring child to term
  • Temilola Akinbolagbe died from OHSS — reminder women bear primary/fatal physical risks
  • "Moral pioneer" burden — women feel they must exhaust every possibility
Key ART Ethics Trap ART greatest advances have potential to SUPPRESS women's autonomy when mismanaged — not inherently liberating!
📖 Benefit and Harm Framework (Francis, 2015)
TheoryDefinition of Harm
Liberal Legal & Political"Setback to interests" — includes posthumous harms
Comparative CounterfactualPerson is worse off than they otherwise would have been
DeontologicalViolation of rights or autonomy, even if it helps health
Bad State AccountBeing in pain/disability/deformity regardless of alternatives
Benatar's Asymmetry: Presence of harm is bad; absence is good. Presence of benefit is good; absence of benefit is NOT bad (no one exists to miss it). Therefore, it is "better never to have been born."
2.07 Palliative Care & Advance Care Planning
What palliative care really is, ACP core components, good death perspectives
🔑 Most Common Misconception = Most Tested! Palliative care is NOT end-of-life care. It is NOT giving up. It is NOT euthanasia. It is NOT limited to dying patients. It CAN be given alongside curative treatment at ANY stage.

✅ Palliative Care IS

  • Interdisciplinary approach
  • Addresses suffering (physical, psychological, social, spiritual)
  • Appropriate at ANY stage of serious illness
  • May occur ALONGSIDE disease-directed treatment
  • NOT defined by prognosis
  • Grounded in beneficence, best interest, proportionality

❌ Palliative Care is NOT

  • Hospice (key diff = timing and scope of ethical legitimacy)
  • Giving up or treatment failure
  • Euthanasia or assisted dying
  • Limited to the dying patient
  • Requiring stopping curative treatment
📋 Advance Care Planning (ACP) — Core Components
ACP is NOT a document alone, NOT a single conversation — it is an ONGOING PROCESS. It begins early when the patient still has full capacity. Ethical foundation = respect for autonomy.
ComponentDescription
1. Values ClarificationClarify which outcomes are acceptable/unacceptable. Ask directly: "Gusto niyo ba ito o ayaw niyo?"
2. Goals of CareDetermine primary goal: life prolongation, functional recovery, OR comfort care only
3. Surrogate Decision-MakerIdentify who will make decisions if patient loses capacity
4. DocumentationProperly document values, goals, and designated surrogate
5. Ongoing CommunicationContinue discussions over time as goals and preferences may change
ACP + Dementia: Precedent autonomy matters. Best-interest standard when wishes are unknown. Ethical tension between past wishes, present condition, and family preferences is UNAVOIDABLE but clear guidance helps.
🕊️ What is a Good Death? — Multiple Perspectives

Patient's Perspective

  • Free from total pain: Physical, Emotional, Social/Relational, Financial/Economic

Family's Perspective

  • No unnecessary pain
  • Everything has been done
  • Home death (increasing favorability)
  • Doctors align family with patient's wishes

Physician's Perspective

  • Follow patient's wishes
  • All options exhausted
  • No unnecessary pain
  • Challenge: futility vs family demands
Key Principle: Delayed ACP = Ethical Failure Most end-of-life conflicts arise from unclear or unknown patient values — NOT from lack of clinical information or resource disagreement. ACP prevents this. Delayed ACP shifts emotional and moral burden to families.

2.08 End-of-Life Issues
DNR, withdrawing/withholding, medical futility, euthanasia & assisted suicide
🧠 Definition of End-of-Life
⚠️ Medical vs Lay Definition Non-medical persons → Cardiac death = EOL
Medical persons → Brain death = EOL (irreversible cessation of ALL functions of entire brain including brainstem, confirmed by clinical exam + apnea testing) — even if mechanical ventilation maintains organ perfusion
✨ "Good Death" Criteria (U of Minnesota)
  • Adequate pain and symptom management
  • Avoid prolonged dying
  • Clear communication
  • Adequate preparation (funeral plans, will)
  • Sense of control
  • Sense of completion
  • Patient is unique
  • Strengthening relationships
  • Not being alone
🔄 Competence vs Capacity — Critical Distinction

Capacity (Clinical)

  • → Determined by physician at bedside
  • → Task-specific, can change
  • → Can have capacity WITHOUT verbal ability
  • → Deafness/mutism does NOT negate capacity

Competence (Legal)

  • → Determined by courts/law
  • → Based on age of majority
  • → A 16-year-old managing own care = incompetent (legal) WITH capacity (clinical)
🚫 DNR Orders — Key Rules
⚠️ #1 Exam Rule A valid DNR signed by a patient WITH decision-making capacity MUST be honored — even if family disagrees, even if family is next of kin. Family cannot override a competent patient's prior directive.
  • Unlike movies, CPR rarely leads to good outcomes in reality
  • DNR must be updated upon each admission (circumstances change)
  • Doctors must guide patients in completing DNR forms
  • Voluntary euthanasia: patient clearly consents to intentionally ending life — illegal in PH

💰 Medical Futility
🔑 "When is enough, enough?" Medical futility arises when treatment is highly unlikely to provide meaningful benefit. The ethical question is NOT about money — it is "will this treatment meaningfully benefit the patient?"

Private Healthcare

  • Patient has right to use their money
  • BUT: physicians are NOT obligated to provide non-beneficial treatment even if patient can pay

Socialized Healthcare

  • Resources from shared tax pool
  • Distributive justice: is it ethical to spend large resources on one patient when pool could benefit many?
"Tama na. Enough." Shift toward: comfort care → palliative care → dignified dying → alternative programs aligned with patient goals
⚰️ Euthanasia vs Assisted Suicide

Euthanasia

  • → Physician DIRECTLY PERFORMS the act causing death
  • → E.g., lethal injection administered by doctor
  • → Deliberate act to end life and relieve suffering
  • → ILLEGAL in Philippines

Assisted Suicide

  • → Patient PERFORMS the act causing death
  • → Using means provided by physician (e.g., prescribed lethal medication, patient self-administers)
  • → Patient may lack access/knowledge/physical ability

⚠️ Depression & Assisted Suicide Severely depressed patients requesting assisted suicide → decision-making capacity is QUESTIONABLE. Untreated depression may impair autonomy → capacity must be evaluated FIRST before proceeding.

🔑 "Always" Rules (Exam-Critical)
  • Valid DNR with capacity → ALWAYS honor (family cannot override)
  • Patient regains capacity → ALWAYS present autonomy prevails over substituted judgment
  • Cannot verbalize/deaf/mute → does NOT mean lack of capacity (communication can occur by other means)
  • Severely depressed requesting AS → ALWAYS assess capacity FIRST
  • Palliative care ≠ giving up → CAN coexist with curative treatment
  • ACP → start EARLY, even while patient has full capacity
  • EOL conflicts → Most often from UNCLEAR PATIENT VALUES, not resource disagreement
❌ "Never" Rules (Exam-Critical)
  • NEVER use cardiac death as medical EOL standard (= brain death)
  • NEVER let family override a valid DNR from competent patient
  • NEVER assume deafness/mutism = lack of capacity
  • NEVER say AI introduces entirely new biases (it echoes existing ones)
  • NEVER equate palliative care with euthanasia/giving up
  • NEVER allow family to override present autonomous patient decision (substituted judgment is inferior)
  • NEVER perform designer baby screening without medically indicated purpose
  • NEVER say BIS = highest standard (it is the LOWEST/last resort)
