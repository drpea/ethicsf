Ethics definition, features, what ethics is not, moral arguments
5 Features of Ethics (Kerridge)
FeatureMeaning
Flourishing & Well-being ⭐Improve life for everyone; not just self
PrescriptiveTells what we should do; imperative language
SystematicUses reason; avoids logical fallacies
UniversalizableApplies equitably to all persons
Overriding importanceAbove law, policy, personal interest
3 Divisions of Ethics
DivisionScope
Meta-ethicsMeaning of ethical terms ("right," "good")
Normative ethicsFrameworks & principles to guide behavior
Practical ethicsApplied: bioethics, nursing ethics, etc.
Structure of a Moral Argument
1
Non-moral premise (factual/descriptive) — e.g., "Trans fat is linked to cardiovascular disease"
2
Moral premise (evaluative) — e.g., "Trans fat is bad for your health"
3
Moral conclusion — e.g., "Trans fat should be banned"
Must have BOTH factual AND evaluative premises to reach a moral conclusion (fact-value gap)
Common Mistakes in Moral Reasoning
FallacyDescriptionExample
Begging the QuestionAssuming what must be proved"Cells can't be a person" (assumes size = personhood)
Ad HominemAttack the person, not the argumentCalling someone a "baby-killer" instead of addressing their argument
Reductio Ad AbsurdumShows argument leads to absurd conclusion"If animals are persons, we'd baptize embryos"
Fact-Value GapMoral conclusion from only factual premisesIgnoring moral assumptions when discussing contraception
Non SequiturConclusion doesn't follow from premises"I'm a med student → therefore I will be a doctor"
Kerridge's Ethical Problem-Solving Framework ⭐
1
Identify the ethical problem — define & do initial conceptual analysis
2
Get the facts — listen to stakeholders; work out what you need to know
3
Consider ethical concepts/theories — identify & critically analyze
4
Think from other perspectives — stakeholders, other theories, precedents
5
Identify ethical conflicts — conflicts between principles; how to resolve
6
Consider legal aspects
7
Identify a way forward — pick option, justify, communicate, document, evaluate
Mill's Harm Principle — Delimiting Moral Issues
DomainDefinitionExample

MoralityActions affecting other peopleLying, promise-breaking
Public PolicyActions affecting others — discouraged or encouragedTobacco taxes; adoption incentives
LegalityActions forbidden or encouraged by lawFines for littering; penalties for drunk driving

Kant's Categorical Imperative
  • Act only on rules everyone can universally agree to
  • Treat people as an END, NEVER merely as means
  • Make laws that allow people to make their own choices
Key quote: "Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or that of another, always as an end and never as a means only."
Alternative Ethical Theories ⭐
TheoryKey IdeaComparisonCriticism
Casuistry Case-based; compare to precedent cases Similar to deontology (generalizes rules) BUT rules from empirical observation, not pure reason Can lead to moral relativism; not a coherent theory
Ethics of Care Relationships > principles; feminist origin (Carol Gilligan); rejects "male" voice Branch of virtue ethics; prevalent in nursing ethics Not fully action-guiding; incomplete for strangers
Principalism Apply 4 principles (Beauchamp & Childress); Autonomy, Beneficence, Non-maleficence, Justice Opposite of casuistry (starts from principles) Doesn't tell us how to rank competing principles
Feminist Ethics Whose voices are silenced? Power imbalances; social structures Overlaps care ethics + critique of hierarchy/oppression —
Virtue Ethics in Medicine
Good physician virtues:
Compassion · Knowledge of healing · Human relation skills
  • Eudaimonia = human flourishing; highest goal
  • Right action = what a virtuous person would do
  • Doctrine of the Mean: virtue is balance between extremes (e.g., courage = between cowardice and recklessness)
Moral VirtuesIntellectual Virtues
Character & attitudes (courage, patience)Thought & reason
Criticism: Circular reasoning — "virtue = how a virtuous person acts"
Assessing Ethical Theories — 5 Criteria
1
Internally consistent — Does it avoid contradicting itself?
2
Logical arguments — Are premises true, yielding true conclusions?
3
Consistent with common morality — Does it align with what we think is right?
4
Face validity — Does it explain the moral world at first glance?
5
Utility — Does it give practical guidance?

10 Nuremberg Code Principles (Summary)
  1. Voluntary consent is absolutely essential
  2. Must yield fruitful results for society; not random/unnecessary
  3. Based on prior animal experiments
  4. Avoid all unnecessary physical/mental suffering
  5. No experiment if death/disabling injury expected (except when researchers are also subjects)
  6. Risk must not exceed humanitarian importance
  7. Adequate facilities to protect subjects
  8. Only scientifically qualified persons
  9. Subject has liberty to end experiment at any time
  10. Scientist must be prepared to terminate experiment
Belmont Report — 3 Core Principles
PrincipleKey Idea
Respect for PersonsAcknowledge autonomy; protect those with diminished autonomy
BeneficenceMaximize benefits, minimize harm
JusticeFair distribution of research benefits and burdens
→ Basis for the Common Rule and establishment of IRBs
Tuskegee Syphilis Study — High-Yield Facts
  • Duration: 40 years (1932–1972)
  • Population: African American men with syphilis
  • Deliberate withholding of treatment (penicillin became available but was not given)
  • Purpose: study disease progression
  • Emblematic of racial discrimination in healthcare
  • Created lasting mistrust of medical institutions in African Americans
  • Led to public outrage → reforms in informed consent + ethical oversight
  • Triggered creation of Belmont Report
Contemporary Ethical Issues in Research
Informed Consent
Expanded; includes explicit right to withdraw at any time
Vulnerable Populations
Children, prisoners, economically disadvantaged
Cultural Sensitivity
Local customs must be respected in diverse populations
Reproducibility Crisis
Replication of studies to confirm findings
Data Privacy & AI
Consent for data use; ethics of AI in research
Global Health
Equity in low-resource settings; WHO/CIOMS guidelines

Relational Autonomy — Key Features
  • Self is socially embedded, not atomistic
  • Autonomy depends on relational competencies: emotional awareness, imaginative capacity, recognition by others, understanding
  • Requires supportive conditions: trust + respect
  • "Man does not live alone; he lives in relation to someone else always"
Asian Context:
Individual is a "smaller self within a larger self"
Family sovereignty > Self-sovereignty
Familial autonomy > Individual autonomy
Collective responsibility is the norm
Applying Autonomy Types to a Refusal (Algorithm)
D
Decisional: Is the patient competent, informed, and voluntary? → If YES, respect refusal
C
Conscientious: Does refusal align with long-term values & commitments? → Respect ongoing moral agency
L
Libertarian: Is refusal free from interference? → Refusal is absolute right (as long as no harm to others)
R
Relational: Assess social context, barriers, support systems → Address structural factors; enable informed choice
⭐ KEY: Different autonomy models → different ethical responses to the same case
Decisional Autonomy Criteria (Christman)
  • Intentional action
  • Competent — ability to understand
  • Voluntary — no coercion or manipulation
  • Person would accept decision upon sustained reflection without resistance/alienation
Focuses on local (everyday decisions), not global (life as a whole) autonomy
Libertarian Autonomy Core Principles
  • Non-threat — as long as you don't harm others
  • Freedom of choice
  • Sovereignty of body — "my body, my rules"
Criticism: Ignores structural inequality & power. Assumes more choices = more freedom. Doesn't ask if choices are authentic or coerced.

1.06 Truth-Telling in Medicine
Theoretical justifications, SPIKES model, confidentiality, exceptions
4 Theoretical Justifications for Truth-Telling ⭐
TheoryCore IdeaKey Point
Deontology (Kant)Categorical DutyLying is always morally wrong regardless of consequences; patient has the right to know
Consequentialism (Mill)Outcome-basedTruth builds trust and leads to better outcomes; deception undermines trust and worsens outcomes
Virtue Ethics (Pellegrino & Thomasma)Character-basedA virtuous physician embodies honesty, integrity, fidelity; a physician who lies is not virtuous
Care Ethics (O'Neill)Context-sensitive, relationalTruth-telling is not a blunt instrument; must be tailored to patient's situation, values, culture; being "compassionately honest"
SPIKES Model for Breaking Bad News ⭐
S
Setting — Quiet, private place; sit down; do not rush
P
Perception — Ask what the patient already knows
I
Invitation — Ask how much detail the patient wants
K
Knowledge — Share truth clearly and gently; plain language; check understanding
E
Emotion — Create space for patient emotions; respond with empathy
S
Strategy & Summary — Plan next steps with patient; provide support
Confidentiality — Grounded In
  • Autonomy
  • Trust (patient may withhold info if trust is broken)
  • Privacy
  • Professional oath
Both truth-telling & confidentiality are prima facie duties — binding but NOT absolute
Exceptions to Confidentiality ⭐
ExceptionExample
Serious risk of harm to othersTarasoff case — duty to warn third party threatened by patient
Public health reportingTB, HIV (RA 11166 in PH)
Legal mandatesChild abuse, subpoenas
Truth-Telling Case Algorithm ⭐
1
Assess patient's prior expressed wishes (e.g., clinic notes, advance directive)
2
Check social media/information environment — could patient discover on own?
3
Consider patient's "right not to know" — some patients prefer not to be told; respect this as autonomy
4
Use SPIKES model for delivery — ensure family support is present
5
If patient prefers not to know: document, and encourage revisiting; involve family with patient's permission
⭐ Truth must eventually be disclosed in a way that honors autonomy while ensuring support
1.07 Decision Making for Adults
Autonomy, surrogate decision-making, advance directives, competence
Surrogate Decision-Making Algorithm ⭐
1
Is the patient competent to make decisions? → If YES → respect their autonomous decision
2
If NO → Is there an advance directive? → If YES → follow it
3
If NO advance directive → Is there a surrogate? → Use surrogate hierarchy
4
Does surrogate know patient's preferences? → If YES → Substituted Judgment ("What would patient have chosen?")
5
Preferences UNKNOWN → Best Interest Standard (maximize benefit, minimize harm)
6
No surrogate (unbefriended) → Attending physician acts as surrogate
Surrogate Hierarchy: Spouse → Adult children → Parents → Siblings → Attending physician (unbefriended)
Substituted Judgment vs Best Interest
Substituted JudgmentBest Interest
WhenPreferences KNOWNPreferences UNKNOWN
Question"What would the patient have chosen?""What is best for the patient?"
BasisPatient's prior values, wishesMaximize benefit, minimize harm; quality of life, dignity
Ethical principleRespects autonomyBeneficence + non-maleficence
Advance Directives
  • Written instructions expressing patient desires for end-of-life care
  • Based on precedent autonomy
  • Becomes active when patient loses decision-making capacity
  • Written and signed; no notarization required (PH context)
  • Releases emotional burden from potential surrogates
Must be validated before honoring (e.g., case of patient with living will in ER)
Competence Assessment ⭐
Patient is competent if they can:
1. Understand the procedure/treatment
2. Weigh risks and benefits
3. Make and communicate a decision based on understanding
  • Task-specific and variable over time
  • Generally assumed competent unless doubts arise
  • Formal assessment required if patient actively refuses
ThresholdRisk
High thresholdWrongly labels competent patients as incompetent
Low thresholdAllows incompetent decisions
Sliding scaleAdjusts by seriousness — but risks paternalism
Baerøe's solution: Collective deliberation — include healthcare workers, surrogates, and patient advocates
Autonomy Challenges to Consent (Elliott)
ChallengeDescription
Lack of competenceSurrogate required
Competent but "irrational"Poor decisions when depressed; still must be respected unless truly incompetent
Problematic identityNeurological damage, dementia — who is the "real" person deciding?
Moral imaginationDifficulty imagining future states (e.g., disability) when making advance decisions
3.01 Commodification: Organ/Tissue Sale & Transplant Tourism
Commodification, instrumentalization, organ allocation, living donors
Commodification — 3 Essential Characteristics ⭐
1. Has a price
Every commodity can be bought and sold
2. Fungible
Interchangeable with others of like type and quality
3. Instrumental value
Valuable only as a means to another end; not intrinsically valuable
Descriptive vs Normative Commodification
DescriptiveNormative
Social practice where things are bought and soldMoral disapproval; the wrong of commodification
Observation, not judgmentArgues it is wrong to treat certain things as commodities
Ex: Organ sales exist in some countriesEx: Organ sales are wrong because they commodify the human body
Proper vs Wrongful Commodities
Proper CommoditiesWrongful Commodities
Truly fungible and only instrumentally valuableTreated as commodities despite NOT being truly fungible
Nothing wrong with treating them as interchangeableViolates inherent value and dignity
Ex: A peso is essentially the same as another pesoEx: Human organs; persons
Instrumentalization (Kant) ⭐
  • Treating a person as a means to an end, not an end in itself
  • Displacement Thesis: Use perspective displaces personhood perspective → undermines status as persons
  • Selling body parts reduces person to a commodity; disregards inherent value
  • Commodification requires instrumentalization
  • Instrumentalization occurs when person is used without valid consent
Use PerspectivePersonhood Perspective
View people as tools or means to an endView people's worth and dignity
Kantian Principle of Respect: Patients must be seen for their inherent value in themselves, not as means to medical/scientific ends
Fungibility (Wilkinson) ⭐
Marshall (1999)Wilkinson (2007)
Fungibility is morally unproblematic in specific contextsIn certain contexts we OUGHT to treat people as non-fungible
A well-trained waiter is just as good as anotherA genuine friendship values the specific friend, not anyone similar
Sale of Body PartsReproductive Ethics
Impersonal relationship → fungibility less relevantPersonal relationship → fungibility more relevant
Vendors & CustomersParent & Children
Organ Allocation — 3 Principles ⭐
Utility
Most benefit to recipient
Urgency
Sickest patients first
Equity
Longest waiting time
⭐ Classic conflict: Patient A (waiting longest, lower survival benefit) vs Patient B (newly listed, higher survival benefit) → Utility vs Equity
  • Restricted from discrimination based on social worth, sex, religion, race
  • "Time on list" is transparent but ignores socioeconomic barriers to listing
Organ Retrieval — Double Veto System ⭐
1
Deceased Veto: If person refused while alive → NO retrieval
2
Family Veto: Even if deceased consented → family can veto → NO retrieval
3
Default: No expressed wishes → family permission usually sufficient
Only ~1% of deaths are suitable for organ donation

Living Donors — Valid Consent Issues ⭐
Valid consent must be: Voluntary + Informed + Competent

Competence is questionable if donor is:
  1. Desperate to save a relative
  2. Subject to moral responsibility
  3. Subject to family pressure
IssueValid?
"Sense of duty"VALID motivation
Coercion/ostracism by familyINVALIDATES consent
Psychosocial screening looks for: excessive sense of duty, undue influence, unconscious neurotic influences, abnormal emotional involvement
Self-Inflicted Illness & Allocation — Arguments
ArgumentClaimRebuttal
Medical ArgumentDeprioritize risk-takers because they may have poorer outcomesFactually untrue that a class would do so badly they get deprioritized
Incentives/Punishment ArgumentDeny organs to smokers/drinkers to discourage behaviorHealthcare is not a tool for punishment; organ failure is already a massive incentive to be healthy
Restoration ArgumentRisk-takers shouldn't harm non-risk-takers by consuming scarce resourcesIrony: if smokers die younger and cost system less, logic would suggest higher priority
All three arguments are generally rejected in ethical practice
State Paternalism vs Autonomy in Organ Donation ⭐
State Paternalism:
Restriction of individual freedom justified by claim of safety.
State says NO to donation of organs essential to life (e.g., heart, second kidney)
Moral Force of Consent Debate:
Can a parent consent to donating their heart (causing their own death) to save a child?
→ Autonomy vs. state paternalism
→ Risk: consent in extreme cases is never truly voluntary
